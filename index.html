Overview

In fields such as natural language processing and computer vision, large models trained on diverse datasets have shown remarkable success in effectively handling various applications and tasks. Traditionally, developing motion control and planning policies for robots has been limited to specific robots and tasks, requiring separate training for each new embodiment and assignment. The ideal goal is to have a single policy applicable to all robots and tasks, but this is challenging due to the limited data available for robotics. This workshop explores the feasibility of creating a "generalist" X-embodiment and X-task policy capable of efficiently adapting to new robots, tasks, and environments. In this workshop, we are not only focused on the sim-to-real challenge, scaling, data collection, and continuous improvements, which were the focuses of previous similar workshops [1, 2, 3], but we are also interested in understanding how neural systems in biological organisms tackle these problems and how classical robotics can contribute to these solutions. Our aim is to bridge insights from neuroscience, robotics, and computer science.

  • In which situations can cross-embodiment help?
  • What role does task abstraction play in enabling robots to adapt their learning to new tasks?
  • Choosing Between an End-to-End Large Model, and Hierarchical Self-Organized Layers?
  • How can we scale robotics data (real-world and/or sim data)?
  • Do we really need generalist or specialist policies?
  • How can Neuroscience and Biology help?
